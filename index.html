Polygamy — more precisely polygyny — is the practice of a man being married to multiple women simultaneously. It has been documented across many cultures and time periods throughout human history. Between approximately 1830 and 1904, the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints practiced polygamy, initially in secret among a small circle of early members, and later more openly as the doctrine became publicly acknowledged.

This website explores the history of polygamy within the early Latter-day Saint church, from its theological foundations to its official discontinuation. The pages here examine the biblical and scriptural basis for the practice, its introduction and development in the early church, the lives of those who lived it, and the political and legal pressures that ultimately led to its end.

Scriptural Background

The Book of Mormon addresses plural marriage directly in the book of Jacob. While Jacob 2:27 establishes monogamy as the Lord's standard — "Wherefore, my brethren, hear me, and hearken to the word of the Lord: for there shall not any man among you have save it be one wife" — verse 30 introduces a significant qualification: "For if I will, saith the Lord of Hosts, raise up seed unto me, I will command my people; otherwise they shall hearken unto these things." This conditional clause became a foundational scriptural justification for plural marriage, framing it not as a contradiction of God's law, but as an exception granted by divine command for a specific purpose.

Doctrine & Theology

Central to understanding polygamy in the early church is the broader theological concept of the Restoration — the belief that Joseph Smith was called to restore all things that had been lost from the ancient church and gospel. Early Latter-day Saint theology pointed to the patriarchs of the Old Testament, Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, as men who practiced plural marriage under God's sanction. In this framework, restoring polygamy was not innovation but recovery of an ancient, divinely authorized order.

Obedience to God's commands, even when difficult or socially unconventional, was also a recurring theme in early Latter-day Saint thought. The willingness to practice plural marriage was often framed as a test of faith — a demonstration of one's commitment to follow God's word regardless of personal comfort or public opinion. This theme of obedience, tied directly to the Abrahamic tradition of sacrifice, gave the practice deep theological weight for those who accepted it.
