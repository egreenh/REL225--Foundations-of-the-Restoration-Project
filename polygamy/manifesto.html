Federal Pressure and the Road to 1890

By the 1880s, the United States federal government had escalated its efforts to eliminate plural marriage among the Latter-day Saints. The Edmunds Act of 1882 made polygamous cohabitation a federal crime, disenfranchising those who practiced it and barring them from jury service and public office. This was followed by the far more sweeping Edmunds-Tucker Act of 1887, which dissolved the Church as a legal institution, authorized the federal government to seize Church property, and removed the right of women in Utah to vote. Together, these measures placed the Church under severe legal and financial strain, threatening its very existence as an institution.

It was in this context that Wilford Woodruff, then serving as President of the Church, issued what became known as the Manifesto in 1890. President Woodruff stated that he had received a revelation directing him to act, framing the decision in terms of the "temporal salvation" of the Church. The coincidence of the timing, a divine directive arriving precisely as the Church faced institutional collapse, has not been lost on historians. It is worth noting, however, that within Latter-day Saint theology this is not unusual; revelation has frequently come in response to pressing circumstances, and the question of whether a revelation is spiritually genuine is not considered to be answered by whether it was also practically necessary.

What the Manifesto Said

The Manifesto itself was relatively brief. In it, President Woodruff declared his intention to submit to the laws of the land regarding marriage and called on Latter-day Saints to refrain from contracting any marriages forbidden by the law of the land. It was presented to and sustained by the general membership of the Church at the general conference of October 1890, and was later canonized as Official Declaration 1 in the Doctrine and Covenants.

After the Manifesto

The Manifesto did not immediately end the practice of plural marriage. In the years following 1890, several hundred plural marriages were performed in and around the United States. This ongoing practice prompted a second, more decisive action in 1904, when President Joseph F. Smith issued a second manifesto, which further prohibited plural marriages, under the threat of being excommunicated.

Today, plural marriage is officially prohibited by the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, and any member found to be practicing it is subject to excommunication. Some fundamentalist groups, often broadly referred to as fundamentalist Mormons, continue to practice plural marriage, though these groups have no affiliation with the mainstream Church and are not recognized by it.
