Plural marriage is not unique to the Latter-day Saint tradition. The Old Testament contains numerous accounts of patriarchs, judges, kings, and ordinary Israelites who practiced polygamy, often without moral censure from the text itself. The following references document instances of plural marriage throughout the biblical record, and formed part of the scriptural foundation that early Latter-day Saints drew upon when understanding and justifying the practice in their own time.

Reference Figure Commentary
Genesis 4:19 Lamech Lamech, a descendant of Cain, had two named wives: Adah and Zillah — one of the earliest recorded instances of plural marriage in scripture.
Genesis 16:1–6 Abraham Abraham is depicted as concurrently having a wife, Sarai, and a concubine, Hagar. This arrangement is presented as initiated by Sarai herself and sanctioned by God.
Genesis 26:34–35, 28:6–9 Esau Esau is depicted as having taken multiple non-Israelite wives, a source of grief to his parents Isaac and Rebekah.
Genesis 29:15–35 Jacob Jacob is depicted as concurrently having two wives, Leah and Rachel, and two concubines, Bilhah and Zilpah — the twelve sons born of these four women became the twelve tribes of Israel.
Genesis 36:9–13 Eliphaz Eliphaz, a son of Esau, had both a wife and a concubine.
1 Chronicles 2:18–20, 46–48 Caleb Caleb, great-grandfather of Bezalel — the chief architect of the Mosaic tabernacle — had three wives and two concubines.
1 Chronicles 4:1–8 Ashur Ashur, a descendant of Judah, had two wives.
Judges 8:29–31 Gideon Gideon, one of the judges of Israel, had seventy sons from multiple wives, as well as a concubine.
1 Samuel 1:1–8 Elkanah Elkanah, father of the prophet Samuel, had two wives — Hannah and Peninnah. The text depicts the tension between the two wives with candor.
1 Samuel 25:39–43, 2 Samuel 5:13–16, 12:7–8 David David is depicted as taking multiple wives and concubines. Notably, in 2 Samuel 12:7–8, the Lord speaks through the prophet Nathan and explicitly states that he gave David his wives.
1 Kings 11:1–8 Solomon Solomon is depicted as having a royal harem of 700 wives and 300 concubines. The text, however, is critical — noting that his foreign wives turned his heart toward other gods.
1 Kings 20:1–12 Ahab Ahab, a king of Israel noted for his wickedness, is said to have had an unspecified number of wives.
2 Chronicles 11:18–23 Rehoboam Rehoboam, a king of Judah and son of Solomon, had eighteen wives and sixty concubines.
2 Chronicles 13:21–22 Abijah Abijah, a king of Judah, had fourteen wives.
2 Chronicles 24:1–3 Joash Joash, the eighth king of Judah, had two wives, both given to him by the priest Jehoiada.
Esther 1–2 Ahasuerus The Persian king Ahasuerus had both a royal harem and multiple wives, including Vashti and later Esther.
Matthew 2 Herod Herod, the Jewish king of Judea mentioned in the nativity account, is known from historical sources outside the New Testament to have practiced polygamy, having had ten wives over the course of his life.

Table adapted from MormonR: Latter-day Saints and Polygamy. Commentary expanded by the author.

It is worth observing that the biblical treatment of plural marriage is not uniform. Some figures, such as David, are depicted as receiving their wives as a divine gift. Others, such as Solomon, are criticized — not for the practice of plural marriage itself, but for taking foreign wives who led him to worship other gods. The moral weight of the text falls less on the structure of the marriage and more on the faithfulness of the individual to the covenant.
