Polygamy in Kirtland and Nauvoo

The origins of plural marriage in the early church trace back to Joseph Smith's study of the Old Testament, specifically his questions regarding the polygamous practices of the ancient patriarchs. As recorded in D&C 132:1, the revelation came in direct response to that inquiry: "Verily, thus saith the Lord unto you my servant Joseph, that inasmuch as you have inquired of my hand to know and understand wherein I, the Lord, justified my servants Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, as also Moses, David and Solomon, my servants, as touching the principle and doctrine of their having many wives and concubines." The revelation further grounded the practice in the examples of the Old Testament patriarchs, as in D&C 132:34-38, which recounts how Abraham, Sarah, and Hagar's arrangement was initiated by God: "God commanded Abraham, and Sarah gave Hagar to Abraham to wife. And why did she do it? Because this was the law; and from Hagar sprang many people. This, therefore, was fulfilling, among other things, the promises... Abraham received concubines, and they bore him children; and it was accounted unto him for righteousness."

Joseph Smith's introduction to the principle was not without personal difficulty. Historical accounts indicate he was hesitant, and early Latter-day Saint tradition holds that an angel appeared to him between 1831 and 1842 commanding him to move forward with the practice or face consequences. The practice began quietly and spread slowly, confined at first to a small circle of trusted associates in Kirtland and later Nauvoo. By June 1844, at the time of Joseph Smith's death, approximately 29 men and 50 women had entered into plural marriage, in addition to Joseph and his own plural wives.

The Nauvoo period also saw the emergence of a troubling counterfeit of the practice. Some men used the language and framework of plural marriage to justify what became known as "spiritual wifery" — unsanctioned sexual relationships dressed in religious language. This was neither endorsed nor tolerated by Joseph Smith or church leadership, and it is important to distinguish this abuse from the doctrinally regulated practice of plural marriage as it was taught and authorized. Church leaders consistently emphasized that any plural marriage required approval from the presiding authorities of the church, and that the practice was governed by covenant and commitment, not personal desire.

It is also worth noting a distinction that is sometimes lost in broader discussions of the practice: not all sealings performed in this period were marriages in the conventional sense. Some were dynastic sealings — performed for eternity only, with no expectation of a conjugal relationship in mortality. These sealings were understood as a way of binding individuals and families together in eternal covenant relationships, and they account for some of the complexity in the historical record around the number of Joseph Smith's plural wives.

Polygamy in Early Utah

When the Saints entered the Salt Lake Valley in 1847, plural marriage came with them. By that point, at least 196 men and 521 women had entered into plural marriages — a significant increase from the Nauvoo period, reflecting both the growth of the practice and its more open acknowledgment among the Saints in their new home. In Utah, plural marriage became a more visible and publicly discussed aspect of Latter-day Saint life, though it remained a topic that required careful navigation both within the community and in relation to the broader American public.

It is a common misconception that all Latter-day Saint men in Utah practiced plural marriage. In reality, while all members were expected to accept plural marriage as a divinely sanctioned principle, not all were expected or required to live it. Practically speaking, the numbers alone would have made universal practice impossible. The decision to enter into plural marriage was understood as a serious covenant undertaking, one that required not only personal willingness but the formal approval of church leadership — a safeguard that, as in Nauvoo, was intended to prevent the kind of abuse that had characterized spiritual wifery.

For those who did practice it, plural marriage was understood primarily as an expression of faith and obedience. It was not presented as an easy or comfortable commandment, and the historical record makes clear that many who lived it — women in particular — found it genuinely difficult. And yet many of those same women recorded their conviction that they were living according to God's will. At its core, the early Latter-day Saint understanding of plural marriage was less about the structure of the family and more about the willingness of the Saints to submit to God's word, even when it demanded something costly.
